================================================================================
SUPPLEMENTAL VETERINARY EMERGENCIES — CRITICAL TRIAGE ADDITIONS
================================================================================
This file adds high-priority emergency topics that are especially important for
dogs and cats in the first minutes before veterinary care is reached.
Always seek professional veterinary treatment as soon as possible.
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: BLOAT / GDV (GASTRIC DILATION-VOLVULUS) IN DOGS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Bloat with possible stomach twisting is a true life-threatening emergency in dogs.
Large and deep-chested breeds are at higher risk, but any dog can be affected.

Warning Signs:
- Suddenly swollen or tight abdomen
- Repeated retching with little or no vomit produced
- Restlessness, pacing, or obvious distress
- Heavy drooling
- Rapid breathing
- Weakness, collapse, or pale gums

What to Do:
1. Treat this as an immediate emergency and contact a veterinary hospital at once.
2. Do NOT give food, water, antacids, or home remedies.
3. Keep your dog as calm and still as possible while arranging transport.
4. Do NOT press on the belly and do NOT try to pass a tube or make the dog vomit.
5. Transport immediately, even if the belly swelling seems to decrease briefly.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: URINARY BLOCKAGE IN CATS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Blocked urination in cats, especially male cats, can become fatal very quickly.

Warning Signs:
- Repeated trips to the litter box with little or no urine produced
- Straining or crying while trying to urinate
- Licking the genital area frequently
- Vomiting, hiding, or sudden lethargy
- Firm, painful abdomen

What to Do:
1. Treat inability to pass urine as an emergency and contact a veterinarian immediately.
2. Do NOT squeeze the bladder or press on the lower abdomen.
3. Keep the cat warm, quiet, and confined for transport.
4. Do NOT give human pain medicines or attempt catheterization at home.
5. Go to an emergency clinic immediately if the cat is weak, vomiting, or collapsing.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: SHOCK IN DOGS AND CATS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Shock can follow trauma, blood loss, heat stroke, poisoning, severe infection,
or allergic reactions.

Possible Signs:
- Pale, white, grey, or blue gums
- Weakness or collapse
- Cold legs or paws
- Rapid heartbeat or very weak pulse
- Fast or shallow breathing
- Dull mentation or unresponsiveness

First Aid for Suspected Shock:
1. Keep your pet as still and quiet as possible.
2. Lay the pet on a flat surface and keep the head level unless breathing is worse in that position.
3. Keep the pet warm with a light blanket, but do not overheat.
4. Do NOT offer food or water if the pet is weak, vomiting, unconscious, or may need sedation.
5. Transport to emergency care immediately.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: SEVERE ALLERGIC REACTION / ANAPHYLAXIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A severe allergic reaction can happen after stings, bites, injections, foods,
or medications.

Warning Signs:
- Sudden facial swelling
- Hives or widespread swelling
- Vomiting or diarrhea soon after exposure
- Trouble breathing or noisy breathing
- Collapse or severe weakness

What to Do:
1. Seek emergency veterinary care immediately if breathing is difficult or the pet collapses.
2. Move the pet away from the suspected trigger if it can be done safely.
3. Keep the airway clear and minimize stress and handling.
4. If a stinger is visible, scrape it away gently with a card-like object rather than squeezing it.
5. Do NOT give human allergy medicines unless your veterinarian specifically instructs you.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: TRAUMA TRANSPORT AND STABILIZATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Pets hit by cars or suffering major falls can have hidden internal injuries even
if they are still awake.

What to Do:
1. Approach carefully because pain can cause even friendly pets to bite or scratch.
2. Control visible bleeding with gentle pressure if possible.
3. Move the pet as little as possible; use a board, blanket, or firm surface to carry them.
4. Keep the spine straight if a back or neck injury is suspected.
5. Transport immediately to emergency veterinary care.
