================================================================================
SUPPLEMENTAL VETERINARY EMERGENCIES — ADVANCED FIRST AID GUIDANCE
================================================================================
This file adds high-priority emergency topics that are especially important for
dogs and cats in the first minutes before veterinary care is reached.
Always seek professional veterinary treatment as soon as possible.
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: HEATSTROKE / HYPERTHERMIA IN DOGS AND CATS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Heatstroke is a severe, life-threatening emergency where a pet's core temperature
rises dangerously high (usually above 104°F / 40°C), often due to hot weather,
being locked in a car, or overexertion.

Warning Signs:
- Excessive, loud, or frantic panting
- Thick, sticky saliva or drooling
- Bright red, purple, or dark gums and tongue
- Rapid heart rate
- Vomiting or bloody diarrhea
- Dizziness, walking in circles, or stumbling (ataxia)
- Confusion, glazed eyes, lethargy, or collapse

What to Do:
1. Move the pet to a cool, shaded, or air-conditioned area immediately.
2. Wet the pet's coat with cool or tepid water (do NOT use freezing cold water or ice water, as this constricts blood vessels and traps heat in the core). Focus on the abdomen, chest, groin, and paw pads.
3. Run a fan over the wet pet to speed up evaporative cooling.
4. Offer fresh, cool water, but do NOT force them to drink. Do NOT leave them unattended.
5. Check their rectal temperature if a thermometer is available. Stop active cooling efforts (remove wet towels and fans) once the temperature drops to 103°F (39.4°C) to prevent rebound hypothermia.
6. Transport the pet to an emergency veterinary clinic immediately, even if they seem to recover, as organ damage can be delayed.

TOPIC: CHOKING AND HEIMLICH MANEUVER FOR PETS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Choking occurs when a foreign object (ball, toy, bone, stick) blocks the airway.
Immediate action is required to save the pet's life.

Warning Signs:
- Frantic pawing at the mouth
- Gagging, coughing, or gasping for air
- Blue or pale tongue and gums (cyanosis)
- Bulging eyes
- Inability to make noise or bark/mew
- Collapse or loss of consciousness

What to Do:
1. Restrain the pet safely but quickly, as a choking pet will panic and may bite.
2. Open the mouth gently and look inside. If the object is clearly visible and loose, perform a finger sweep to remove it. Do NOT perform a blind sweep or push the object further down the throat.
3. For small dogs or cats: Lift the pet with their back against your chest, suspend them, and apply firm, upward abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver) just below their ribcage using your fist, or give firm back blows between the shoulder blades.
4. For large dogs: Place the dog on their side on a firm surface. Place one hand over the other just below the ribcage in the soft part of the belly. Press firmly upward and forward toward the head 5 times.
5. If the object does not come out and the pet collapses, perform chest compressions to attempt to dislodge the object using air pressure.
6. Transport immediately to an emergency veterinary clinic while continuing first-aid efforts.

TOPIC: SEIZURES IN DOGS AND CATS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A seizure is a temporary disturbance of brain function, causing involuntary muscle
activity, shaking, paddling, or loss of awareness.

Possible Signs:
- Sudden collapse and stiffening of the body
- Paddling of the legs
- Uncontrolled chewing, drooling, or foaming at the mouth
- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Vocalizing or whining
- Disorientation, blindness, or pacing before or after the event (post-ictal phase)

What to Do:
1. Do NOT try to restrain the pet or hold their tongue. Pets cannot swallow their tongues, and holding their mouth increases the risk of severe bites.
2. Clear the surrounding area of sharp objects, stairs, or furniture to prevent injury.
3. Turn off bright lights and quiet any loud noises to reduce sensory stimulation.
4. Keep the pet cool, especially if the seizure lasts more than 2 minutes, by wetting their paws and ears with cool water (long seizures raise core temperature).
5. Time the seizure accurately. Keep a log of when it started, how long it lasted, and their behavior before and after.
6. Seek emergency veterinary care immediately if a single seizure lasts longer than 3 minutes, if the pet has multiple seizures in a row (cluster seizures), or if they do not wake up between seizures.

TOPIC: BURNS AND SCALDS (THERMAL AND CHEMICAL)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Burns can result from hot liquids (scalding), open flames, heating pads, cords (electrical burns), or corrosive household chemicals.

Possible Signs:
- Singed hair or red, blistered skin
- Pet licking or scratching a specific painful area
- Swelling or weeping of the skin
- Shock, heavy panting, or whimpering

What to Do:
1. For thermal burns: Flush the burned area with cool, running tap water for at least 20 minutes. Do NOT apply ice, ice water, butter, oils, or ointments, as these trap heat and worsen the tissue damage.
2. For chemical burns: Flush the area with copious amounts of running water for 20-30 minutes to wash away the corrosive substance. Wear gloves to protect yourself.
3. For electrical burns (usually from chewing cords): Safely unplug the cord BEFORE touching the pet. Check if the pet is breathing; if unconscious, look for charring around the gums.
4. Cover the area loosely with a clean, damp cloth or sterile non-stick bandage. Keep the pet warm with a dry blanket over their back to prevent shock.
5. Transport to emergency veterinary care immediately.

TOPIC: DROWNING AND NEAR DROWNING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Drowning can occur in swimming pools, ponds, baths, or floodwaters. Pets can suffer
from lack of oxygen and secondary lung complications hours after the event (secondary drowning).

What to Do:
1. Safely retrieve the pet from the water. Protect yourself from getting pulled in or bitten.
2. Hold the pet with their head lower than their hindquarters (for small dogs or cats, you can hold them by their hind legs briefly; for large dogs, drape them over a solid surface or your shoulder on their side) to allow gravity to drain water from the lungs and airway.
3. Clear any debris, mud, or vomit from their mouth.
4. Check for breathing and a heartbeat. If the pet is not breathing, perform pet CPR (mouth-to-snout rescue breaths and chest compressions).
5. Wrap the pet in dry, warm blankets to treat hypothermia. Do NOT rub them vigorously if they have fractures.
6. Transport the pet to an emergency veterinary clinic immediately, even if they seem to have fully recovered. Water inhaled into the lungs can cause inflammation and fluid accumulation (pulmonary edema) up to 24 hours later.
