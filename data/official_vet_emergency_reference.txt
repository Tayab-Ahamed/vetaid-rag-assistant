TOPIC: UNIVERSAL EMERGENCY TRIAGE FOR DOGS AND CATS
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - What to Do in a Dog or Cat Emergency
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/special-pet-topics/emergencies/what-to-do-in-a-dog-or-cat-emergency
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Use this topic when the owner is unsure whether a problem is an emergency.

Immediate veterinary attention is needed for:
- poisoning
- severe or uncontrolled bleeding
- trouble breathing
- serious eye injuries
- suspected fractures or inability to use a limb
- collapse, unresponsiveness, or major trauma

First-aid principles:
1. Protect the owner first because injured pets may bite or scratch.
2. Call the veterinarian or emergency clinic early so transport and first-aid steps can be guided by phone.
3. Focus first on airway, breathing, and circulation concerns.
4. Keep movement minimal during transport, especially after trauma.
5. Use a flat board, thick cardboard, or a folded blanket to support an injured pet.

TOPIC: EMERGENCY BLEEDING CONTROL
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - What to Do in a Dog or Cat Emergency
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/special-pet-topics/emergencies/what-to-do-in-a-dog-or-cat-emergency
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Helpful when the pet has active external bleeding.

Important signs:
- blood loss that does not slow quickly
- weakness, pale gums, collapse, or rapid breathing
- blood soaking through improvised bandages

What to do:
1. Press firmly on the wound with a clean cloth, towel, or gauze.
2. Apply a firm bandage if possible, but do not make it so tight that it cuts circulation.
3. If blood soaks through, add more material on top instead of removing the first layer.
4. Continue direct pressure during transport.
5. Seek urgent veterinary care if bleeding is severe, persistent, or associated with weakness or collapse.

TOPIC: BURNS, CHEMICAL EXPOSURE, AND SURFACE HEAT INJURY
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - What to Do in a Dog or Cat Emergency
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/special-pet-topics/emergencies/what-to-do-in-a-dog-or-cat-emergency
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Use this topic for thermal burns, chemical burns, and hot-surface paw injuries.

What to do:
1. Remove the pet from the source of heat or chemical exposure.
2. Flush the affected area gently with cool water.
3. Cover the area with a clean nonstick dressing if available.
4. Do not apply butter, toothpaste, harsh cleaners, or home remedies.
5. Face burns, airway burns, electrical burns, large burns, and chemical burns are emergencies.

TOPIC: HEAT STROKE IN DOGS AND CATS
SOURCE: American Red Cross - Dog Heat Stroke
URL: https://www.redcross.org/take-a-class/resources/learn-pet-first-aid/dog/heat-stroke
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - What to Do in a Dog or Cat Emergency
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/special-pet-topics/emergencies/what-to-do-in-a-dog-or-cat-emergency
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency.

Warning signs:
- collapse
- excessive panting or difficulty breathing
- drooling or heavy salivation
- vomiting or bloody diarrhea
- wobbliness, distress, or uncoordinated movement
- very red mucous membranes

Useful temperature guidance:
- normal body temperature for dogs is about 99.5 to 102.5 F
- overheating concern rises when temperature is above 104 F

First aid:
1. Move the pet out of direct heat immediately.
2. Cool with cool water, wet towels, and airflow from a fan.
3. Focus cooling on the head, neck, chest, abdomen, and feet when practical.
4. Do not immerse in ice water or aggressively overcool.
5. Offer small amounts of water only if the pet is conscious and improving.
6. Go to a veterinarian immediately even if the pet seems better.

TOPIC: DOG CHOKING FIRST AID
SOURCE: American Red Cross - What to Do if Your Dog is Choking
URL: https://www.redcross.org/take-a-class/resources/learn-pet-first-aid/dog/choking
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - What to Do in a Dog or Cat Emergency
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/special-pet-topics/emergencies/what-to-do-in-a-dog-or-cat-emergency
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Possible choking signs:
- frantic behavior
- pawing at the mouth
- loud breathing sounds
- gasping
- blue or white gums
- inability to breathe normally

First aid:
1. Open the mouth only if it is safe and look for a visible object.
2. Avoid blindly pushing fingers deep into the mouth.
3. If the object cannot be removed, use abdominal thrusts and back blows as emergency measures.
4. If the dog becomes unresponsive, begin rescue breathing and CPR if needed.
5. Even after the object is removed, the dog still needs immediate veterinary evaluation because choking can injure the airway or lungs.

TOPIC: CAT SEIZURE EMERGENCY GUIDANCE
SOURCE: American Red Cross - Seizures in Cats
URL: https://www.redcross.org/take-a-class/resources/learn-pet-first-aid/cat/seizures
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Use this topic for active seizures and the short recovery period afterward.

What to do:
1. Keep the cat in a safe area and move furniture or hazards away if possible.
2. Do not restrain the cat.
3. Keep hands away from the mouth.
4. Time the seizure if possible.
5. Treat seizures lasting more than 3 to 4 minutes as emergencies.
6. Repeated seizures or poor recovery between seizures need urgent veterinary care.

TOPIC: TOP HOUSEHOLD TOXIN GROUPS FOR PETS
SOURCE: ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center - The Official Top 10 Toxins of 2024
URL: https://www.aspca.org/news/official-top-10-toxins-2024
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ASPCA reported that in 2024, over-the-counter human medications remained the top exposure category and accounted for 16.5 percent of reported exposures.

Common toxin groups to consider in history taking:
- over-the-counter human medicines
- prescription medicines
- human foods and drinks
- chocolate
- veterinary products used incorrectly
- plants and mushrooms
- recreational drugs
- rodenticides and insecticides

Safer first-aid framing:
1. Ask exactly what substance was involved, how much, and when exposure happened.
2. Do not recommend inducing vomiting unless a veterinarian or poison expert instructs it.
3. Keep packaging, photos, or product labels ready for the clinic or poison hotline.
4. Escalate immediately when the pet has tremors, collapse, trouble breathing, repeated vomiting, or altered behavior.

TOPIC: URINARY BLOCKAGE IN MALE CATS
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - Urinary Stones in Cats
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/cat-owners/kidney-and-urinary-tract-disorders-of-cats/urinary-stones-uroliths-calculi-in-cats
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This is one of the most important cat emergencies in a first-aid chatbot.

Typical warning signs:
- frequent trips to the litter box
- straining
- only drops of urine or no urine
- crying in pain
- vomiting
- low energy or hiding

Risk framing:
- complete blockage can cause dangerous toxin buildup within 1 to 2 days
- death may occur within about 3 days if complete obstruction is not relieved

What to do:
1. Treat inability to pass urine as an emergency.
2. Do not squeeze the bladder or press repeatedly on the lower abdomen.
3. Do not give human pain medicine.
4. Transport to a veterinarian or emergency clinic immediately.

TOPIC: BLOAT OR GDV IN DOGS
SOURCE: VCA Animal Hospitals - Bloat: Gastric Dilatation and Volvulus in Dogs
URL: https://vcahospitals.com/know-your-pet/bloat-gastric-dilatation-and-volvulus-in-dogs
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - Gastric Dilation and Volvulus in Small Animals
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/digestive-system/diseases-of-the-stomach-and-intestines-in-small-animals/gastric-dilation-and-volvulus-in-small-animals
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

GDV is a life-threatening emergency in dogs and requires immediate veterinary treatment.

High-risk patterns:
- large or deep-chested dogs
- restlessness or distress after eating
- heavy drooling
- swollen abdomen
- trouble breathing
- repeated retching without producing vomit
- collapse or inability to stand

Important notes:
- VCA describes GDV as one of the most serious non-traumatic emergencies in dogs.
- Dogs over 100 pounds have about a 20 percent lifetime risk of bloat according to the VCA summary.

What to do:
1. Do not give food, water, or home remedies.
2. Do not wait for the abdomen to become obviously huge.
3. Go to an emergency veterinarian immediately.

TOPIC: PET CPR AND TRANSPORT BASICS
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - What to Do in a Dog or Cat Emergency
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/special-pet-topics/emergencies/what-to-do-in-a-dog-or-cat-emergency
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Use this topic only for high-acuity situations where the pet is unconscious or not breathing.

Key points:
- first check chest movement and whether a heartbeat can be felt
- pet CPR starts with chest compressions around 100 to 120 per minute
- if CPR is needed during transport, another person should drive

Transport basics:
1. Keep the head, neck, and spine as still as possible after trauma.
2. Use a firm board, cardboard, or folded blanket to carry the pet.
3. Keep anything from pressing on the neck when breathing is difficult.

TOPIC: EMERGENCY PREPAREDNESS AND FIRST-AID KIT
SOURCE: Merck Veterinary Manual - First Aid Kit for a Pet
URL: https://www.merckvetmanual.com/special-pet-topics/emergencies/first-aid-kit-for-a-pet
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Good first-aid kit items for a dog or cat include:
- muzzle
- gauze, sterile pads, stretch bandage, and tape
- blunt scissors
- saline solution
- tweezers or forceps
- gloves
- thermometer
- lubricating jelly
- a small supply of regular prescribed medications

Preparedness reminders:
1. A first-aid kit is not a substitute for veterinary care.
2. Owners should know how to use the kit items before an emergency happens.
3. Medication expiry dates should be checked regularly.
