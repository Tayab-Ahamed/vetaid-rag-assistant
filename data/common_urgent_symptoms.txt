================================================================================
SUPPLEMENTAL VETERINARY EMERGENCIES — COMMON URGENT SYMPTOMS
================================================================================
This file expands the knowledge base with symptom-focused emergency first-aid
topics commonly asked by pet owners.
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: VOMITING AND DIARRHEA WITH RED FLAGS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Vomiting or diarrhea may be mild, but urgent signs need quick veterinary help.

Urgent Warning Signs:
- Blood in vomit or stool
- Repeated vomiting that prevents the pet from keeping water down
- Severe weakness, collapse, or shaking
- Distended abdomen or repeated unproductive retching
- Known toxin exposure
- Very young, very old, or chronically ill pets becoming dehydrated

What to Do:
1. Remove access to food until you speak with your veterinarian, unless they advise otherwise.
2. Offer only small amounts of water if the pet is alert and not vomiting continuously.
3. Do NOT give human anti-diarrheal or anti-nausea medication unless prescribed by a vet.
4. Save a sample or photo of vomit, stool, or suspected toxin if safe to do so.
5. Seek veterinary care promptly if warning signs are present.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: DIFFICULTY BREATHING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Breathing trouble is always urgent.

Warning Signs:
- Open-mouth breathing in cats
- Loud, harsh, or very rapid breathing
- Blue, purple, or grey gums
- Neck stretched out to breathe
- Collapse or panic

What to Do:
1. Keep your pet calm and avoid unnecessary movement.
2. Do NOT force the pet to lie down if they breathe better sitting or standing.
3. Keep the airway clear and remove tight collars or harnesses.
4. Do NOT put your hands in the mouth of a distressed pet unless there is a clearly visible object causing choking.
5. Go to an emergency veterinarian immediately.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: EYE INJURY OR SUDDEN EYE PROBLEM
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Eye emergencies worsen quickly and should be treated promptly.

Urgent Signs:
- Pawing at the eye
- Eye held shut
- Sudden cloudiness or bleeding
- Bulging eye
- Chemical splash

What to Do:
1. Prevent rubbing by using an e-collar if one is available.
2. If there was a chemical splash, rinse gently with clean lukewarm water or saline for 15 minutes.
3. Do NOT apply human eye drops or ointments unless prescribed for that pet.
4. Do NOT try to push a bulging eye back into place.
5. Seek veterinary attention as soon as possible.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: BITE WOUNDS AND ABSCESS RISK
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Animal bites may look small on the surface but can trap infection deep under the skin.

What to Do:
1. If the wound is bleeding, apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth.
2. Rinse superficial dirt gently with clean water if the pet allows, but do not scrub deeply.
3. Do NOT use hydrogen peroxide repeatedly inside wounds because it can damage tissue.
4. Watch for swelling, pus, pain, fever, or worsening lethargy.
5. Arrange veterinary care because many bite wounds need antibiotics and deeper assessment.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TOPIC: WHEN A SEIZURE BECOMES A TRUE EMERGENCY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A single short seizure can still require a vet visit, but some seizure patterns
need immediate emergency care.

Emergency Patterns:
- The seizure lasts longer than 2 to 3 minutes
- Multiple seizures happen close together
- The pet does not recover between seizures
- The pet has trouble breathing, severe overheating, or serious trauma during the episode

What to Do:
1. Move objects away so your pet cannot hit furniture or stairs.
2. Do NOT place your hands near the mouth and do NOT try to hold the tongue.
3. Dim lights, reduce noise, and time the episode if possible.
4. Keep the pet cool after the seizure if overheating is suspected.
5. Seek emergency care immediately for prolonged or repeated seizures.
