================================================================================
VETERINARY FIRST-AID GUIDE — COMPREHENSIVE REFERENCE DOCUMENT
================================================================================
This document provides emergency first-aid information for common pet situations.
Always seek professional veterinary care as soon as possible.
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: BLEEDING & WOUNDS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: External Bleeding in Dogs and Cats

Bleeding from cuts, lacerations, or puncture wounds is a common emergency.
Quick, calm action can prevent excessive blood loss before you reach a vet.

What to Do When Your Pet Is Bleeding:
1. Stay calm. Your pet senses your anxiety and may become more agitated.
2. Apply gentle pressure using a clean cloth, gauze pad, or a folded towel.
3. Hold pressure firmly for 5 to 10 minutes without lifting the cloth to check—
   lifting removes clotting that has begun to form.
4. If blood soaks through, layer more material on top rather than removing the
   original dressing.
5. For limb wounds, you may apply a light pressure bandage, but never use a
   tourniquet unless trained to do so, as improper use can cause permanent injury.
6. Once bleeding is controlled, keep the wound clean and transport your pet to a
   veterinarian immediately.

Signs That Bleeding Is Serious:
- Blood is bright red and spurting (arterial bleed — extremely urgent)
- Bleeding does not slow within 10 minutes of pressure
- The wound is deep, wide, or exposes tissue or bone
- Your pet is lethargic, pale-gummed, or unresponsive

TOPIC: Internal Bleeding in Pets

Internal bleeding is harder to detect but equally dangerous.

Signs of Internal Bleeding in Dogs and Cats:
- Distended, hard, or painful abdomen
- Pale, white, or bluish gums
- Rapid, shallow breathing
- Weakness, collapse, or inability to stand
- Vomiting blood or passing bloody stool

What to Do:
1. Do NOT give food or water.
2. Keep your pet warm and as still as possible.
3. Minimize handling to avoid increasing internal pressure.
4. Transport immediately to an emergency veterinary clinic.

TOPIC: Nosebleeds (Epistaxis) in Dogs and Cats

Nosebleeds can result from trauma, foreign objects, clotting disorders, or tumors.

What to Do:
1. Keep your pet calm and quiet.
2. Apply a cold pack or ice wrapped in a cloth gently to the bridge of the nose.
3. Do NOT tilt the head back — this causes blood to be swallowed.
4. Do NOT stuff gauze into the nostril.
5. If bleeding lasts more than 5 minutes, seek veterinary help immediately.

SECTION 2: BURNS & HEAT INJURIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Thermal Burns in Dogs and Cats

Burns can result from fire, hot liquids, steam, heated surfaces (e.g., car hoods,
pavement), or chemicals.

Degrees of Burns:
- First Degree: Redness, surface only — least severe
- Second Degree: Blistering, painful, moist-looking skin
- Third Degree: White or charred skin, may be painless (nerve damage)

First Aid for Thermal Burns:
1. Remove your pet from the heat source immediately. Protect yourself first.
2. Cool the burned area with cool (NOT ice-cold) running water for 10-15 minutes.
   Never apply ice, butter, toothpaste, or ointments — these worsen tissue damage.
3. Cover the area loosely with a clean, damp cloth or sterile non-stick bandage.
4. Do not break blisters — this increases infection risk.
5. Keep your pet warm (burns cause body temperature to drop) and transport to a vet.

TOPIC: Chemical Burns in Pets

Chemical burns from household cleaners, acids, or pesticides require prompt action.

What to Do:
1. Wear gloves before touching your pet to avoid chemical transfer to your skin.
2. Brush off any dry chemical powder carefully before adding water.
3. Flush the affected area thoroughly with lukewarm water for at least 15-20 minutes.
4. Do NOT use vinegar, baking soda, or other neutralizers on the skin without vet advice.
5. If the chemical was ingested or got in the eyes, call Poison Control for Pets and
   your vet immediately.

TOPIC: Heat Stroke (Hyperthermia) in Pets

Heat stroke is a life-threatening emergency, especially for flat-faced breeds
(Bulldogs, Persians) and overweight animals.

Signs of Heat Stroke:
- Excessive, frantic panting
- Drooling thick saliva
- Bright red or very pale gums
- Vomiting or diarrhea
- Staggering, seizures, or collapse

What to Do for Heat Stroke:
1. Move your pet to a cool, shaded, or air-conditioned environment immediately.
2. Apply cool (not cold) wet towels to the neck, armpits, and groin areas.
3. Offer small amounts of cool water to drink if conscious — do not force water.
4. Use a fan to increase evaporation cooling.
5. Do NOT submerge in cold water or use ice — this can cause shock and constrict
   blood vessels, trapping heat inside.
6. Transport to a veterinary emergency clinic as quickly as possible — even if your
   pet seems to recover, internal damage may have occurred.

TOPIC: Pavement and Hot Surface Burns on Paws

In summer, pavement, sand, and metal can reach 60°C (140°F) and cause severe paw burns.

What to Do:
1. Remove your pet from the hot surface immediately.
2. Rinse paws with cool water for 10 minutes.
3. Check for blistering, redness, or missing skin between pads.
4. Apply a light bandage if available; carry your pet to avoid further injury.
5. Seek veterinary evaluation for all but the mildest redness.

Prevention: Test pavement with the back of your hand for 7 seconds — if too hot for
you, it is too hot for your pet's paws.

SECTION 3: CHOKING & AIRWAY EMERGENCIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Choking in Dogs

Dogs are prone to choking on bones, toys, balls, or food items.

Signs of Choking in Dogs:
- Pawing at the mouth frantically
- Gagging or retching without producing vomit
- High-pitched wheezing or whistle-like noise when breathing
- Blue or grey-tinged gums (cyanosis — critical)
- Panic, distress, or sudden collapse

First Aid for Choking in a Conscious Dog:
1. Stay calm and approach your dog carefully — a panicked animal may bite.
2. Open the mouth gently. Tilt head slightly back. Look inside for a visible object.
3. If you can CLEARLY see the object, use your fingers or flat-nosed pliers to remove it.
   Do NOT blindly sweep your fingers inside — you may push the object deeper.
4. If the object cannot be easily retrieved, perform abdominal thrusts (Heimlich maneuver):
   - For large dogs standing: kneel behind them, wrap arms around the belly just behind
     the last ribs, and give 3-5 quick, firm upward-forward thrusts.
   - For small dogs: hold them with their back against your chest, place a fist just
     below the ribs, and give 3-5 quick compressions upward.
5. Recheck the mouth after thrusts. Repeat until the object is dislodged or you reach a vet.
6. Even if the object is dislodged, always have your vet examine your pet for throat injury.

First Aid for Choking in an Unconscious Dog:
1. Lay the dog on its side.
2. Open the mouth and look for the object. Remove only if clearly visible.
3. Give 2 rescue breaths through the nose (close the mouth and blow into the nostrils).
4. If no breathing, begin CPR (see CPR section).
5. Rush to emergency vet immediately.

TOPIC: Choking in Cats

Cats are less likely to choke on large objects but may choke on string, thread,
needles, rubber bands, or hair ties.

Signs of Choking in Cats:
- Open-mouth breathing (cats normally breathe through the nose)
- Gagging or repetitive swallowing
- Pawing at face or mouth
- Panic or unusual behaviour
- Blue-tinged lips or tongue

First Aid for Choking in Cats:
1. Wrap the cat in a thick towel to protect yourself from scratches.
2. Gently open the mouth and look for a visible foreign object.
3. If visible and easily reachable, remove with tweezers or your fingers. Never reach in blindly.
4. For small cats, modified Heimlich: hold the cat with its back to your chest. Apply firm
   upward pressure with two fingers just below the ribcage, 3-5 times.
5. Do NOT attempt to pull string or thread out — it may be wrapped around internal organs.
6. Transport to a vet immediately regardless of outcome.

IMPORTANT WARNING: Never attempt a Heimlich maneuver on a pet that is not truly choking.
If your pet can cough, breathe, or vocalize, monitor closely and see a vet promptly.

SECTION 4: POISONING & TOXIC INGESTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Common Pet Poisons — Dogs and Cats

Many everyday household items are toxic to pets. Know these hazards:

Foods Toxic to Dogs:
- Chocolate (theobromine toxicity) — all types, dark chocolate is worst
- Xylitol (artificial sweetener in gum, candy, some peanut butters)
- Grapes and raisins (can cause kidney failure)
- Onions, garlic, leeks, chives (damage red blood cells)
- Macadamia nuts
- Alcohol (even small amounts)
- Avocado (toxic to many animals)
- Coffee, tea, caffeinated drinks
- Raw yeast dough

Foods Toxic to Cats:
- Onions and garlic
- Alcohol
- Xylitol
- Chocolate (less commonly consumed but toxic)
- Raw fish (prolonged feeding destroys Vitamin B1)

Other Common Household Toxins:
- Antifreeze / Ethylene glycol (smells sweet, extremely deadly to pets)
- Rat poison / Rodenticides (often causes internal bleeding)
- Certain plants: Lilies (fatal to cats), Sago Palm, Oleander, Azalea, Tulip bulbs
- Human medications: Ibuprofen, Acetaminophen, Antidepressants, ADD medications
- Insecticides and pesticides
- Bleach, detergents, cleaning products
- Lead paint chips

TOPIC: Signs of Poisoning in Pets

Symptoms vary by toxin but commonly include:
- Vomiting or diarrhea (sometimes bloody)
- Drooling excessively
- Muscle tremors or seizures
- Loss of coordination / stumbling
- Dilated or constricted pupils
- Difficulty breathing
- Collapse or sudden weakness
- Excessive thirst and urination (sign of antifreeze or kidney toxins)
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin/whites of eyes)

TOPIC: First Aid for Poisoning in Dogs and Cats

1. Remove your pet from the source of poison immediately.
2. Do NOT induce vomiting unless specifically instructed to do so by a vet or poison
   control hotline — some toxins (acids, alkalis, petroleum products) cause MORE damage
   coming back up.
3. Collect the following information:
   - What was ingested (bring packaging if possible)
   - Estimated amount ingested
   - Time of ingestion
   - Your pet's weight and species
4. Call your veterinarian or an animal poison control hotline immediately.
   ASPCA Animal Poison Control: +1-888-426-4435 (US)
   Pet Poison Helpline: +1-855-764-7661 (US)
5. If your vet instructs you to induce vomiting (dogs only, not cats):
   - Use 3% hydrogen peroxide: 1 teaspoon per 5 kg (11 lbs) body weight, max 3 teaspoons
   - Administer orally and walk your dog to encourage movement
   - Expect vomiting within 15 minutes
6. Do NOT give milk, water, or any home remedies unless instructed by a vet.
7. Transport to emergency vet with the poison container or a photo of the label.

TOPIC: Skin Contact with Poisons

If your pet walks through a chemical, pesticide, or toxic plant:
1. Prevent them from licking the substance — use an e-collar if available.
2. Rinse affected skin and paws with lukewarm water for 10-15 minutes.
3. Mild dish soap (like Dawn) can help remove oily substances; rinse thoroughly.
4. Do not scrub — this increases absorption through skin.
5. Call your vet to determine if additional treatment is needed.

TOPIC: Toad and Snake Poisoning

Toad Poisoning (Bufo/Cane Toads):
- Dogs often mouth toads and absorb toxins through gums
- Symptoms: Excessive drooling, red gums, pawing at mouth, seizures
- Flush mouth with running water, wiping from back to front to avoid swallowing
- Seek emergency vet care immediately

Snake Bites:
- Keep the animal calm and still to slow venom spread
- Carry rather than walk your pet
- Remove collars if bitten near neck (swelling may occur)
- Do NOT cut, suck, or tourniquet the bite wound
- Get to an emergency vet immediately — time is critical

SECTION 5: FRACTURES & ORTHOPEDIC EMERGENCIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Fractures and Broken Bones in Pets

Fractures are breaks or cracks in bones, commonly caused by trauma (car accidents,
falls from height, kicks). They can be:
- Closed (skin intact) or Open/Compound (bone pierces skin)
- Open fractures are medical emergencies due to infection risk

Signs of a Fracture in Dogs and Cats:
- Sudden inability or refusal to bear weight on a limb
- Visible deformity, unusual angle, or abnormal movement of a leg
- Swelling, bruising, or firm lump at the injury site
- Bone protruding through skin (compound fracture)
- Crying, whimpering, or growling when limb is touched
- Excessive licking or biting at a specific area
- Dragging hindlimbs (possible spinal fracture — extreme emergency)

First Aid for Suspected Fractures:
1. Approach your pet calmly. Even the gentlest animal may bite in severe pain —
   use a muzzle if necessary (do NOT muzzle if vomiting or having difficulty breathing).
2. Minimise movement. Do not attempt to straighten or reposition the broken limb.
3. For transport, gently slide the pet onto a flat, rigid surface (board, tray,
   stiff cardboard) if possible. Use a blanket as a stretcher for larger dogs.
4. If it is a compound fracture (bone visible), cover loosely with a moist, clean cloth.
   Do NOT push the bone back in or apply ointments.
5. You may loosely wrap a supportive dressing around a closed limb fracture to limit
   movement — do NOT apply tight bandages or splints unless you are trained; incorrect
   splinting causes more harm than none.
6. Transport to a veterinarian immediately. Call ahead so the clinic can prepare.

TOPIC: Spinal Injuries in Pets

Spinal or neck injuries can cause permanent paralysis if handled incorrectly.

Signs:
- Inability to move hindlimbs or all four limbs
- Dragging rear legs while front legs work
- Yelping when touched along the back
- Loss of bladder or bowel control

What to Do:
1. Do NOT bend or twist the spine in any way.
2. Slide the animal onto a rigid flat board (like a ironing board or piece of wood)
   as a stretcher, keeping the spine perfectly straight.
3. Secure the animal lightly so it cannot move or fall off.
4. Transport immediately to emergency veterinary care.
5. Do NOT wrap the neck — keep the head in a neutral position.

TOPIC: Dislocations in Pets

Dislocations occur when bones shift out of their normal joint position.

Signs: Abnormal limb posture, severe pain, refusal to move the joint

What to Do:
1. Do NOT attempt to relocate the joint — this requires anesthesia and imaging.
2. Treat exactly like a fracture: immobilise, transport, and seek veterinary care.

TOPIC: Sprains and Soft Tissue Injuries

Limping without obvious bone deformity may indicate a sprain or muscle tear.

What to Do:
1. Rest your pet — no running, jumping, or stairs.
2. Apply a cold pack wrapped in cloth to the area for 10-15 minutes, 2-3 times a day.
3. Do not give human NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin) — they are toxic to pets.
4. A veterinarian can prescribe safe pain relief. Schedule an appointment within 24 hours.

SECTION 6: CARDIOPULMONARY RESUSCITATION (CPR) FOR PETS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Pet CPR — Dogs and Cats

CPR is performed when your pet is unconscious and has no heartbeat or breathing.

Checking for Vital Signs:
1. Tap your pet and call their name — check for responsiveness.
2. Watch for chest movement to assess breathing.
3. Place your hand on the left side of the chest (just behind the elbow) to feel for a heartbeat.
4. Check for a pulse on the inside of the upper hind leg (femoral artery).

Performing CPR on a Dog:
1. Lay the dog on its right side on a firm, flat surface.
2. Position hands: one on top of the other, heel of hand on the widest part of the chest.
3. Compress chest to 1/3 to 1/2 its width at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute.
4. Give 2 rescue breaths after every 30 compressions:
   - Close the mouth tightly. Breathe into the nose until you see the chest rise.
5. Continue until breathing resumes, the heart beats, or you reach a vet.

Performing CPR on a Cat or Small Dog:
1. Lay on right side on a firm surface.
2. Wrap one hand around the sternum (chest); compress with thumb and fingers.
3. Compress at 100-120 per minute, 1/3 depth.
4. Give 2 small breaths through the nose every 30 compressions.

SECTION 7: EYE AND EAR EMERGENCIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Eye Injuries and Emergencies in Pets

Common causes: scratches, foreign objects, chemical splashes, eye infections.

Signs of Eye Emergency:
- Squinting, pawing at the eye
- Redness, swelling, or discharge
- Cloudy or bluish appearance to the cornea
- Visible foreign body
- Eye bulging from socket (proptosis — emergency)

First Aid for Eye Injuries:
1. Prevent your pet from rubbing the eye — use an e-collar if available.
2. For chemical splash: rinse the eye immediately with saline solution or clean water
   for 10-15 minutes, directing flow from the inner corner outward.
3. Do NOT try to remove embedded objects from the eye.
4. Do NOT apply eye drops unless prescribed by your vet.
5. Cover the eye loosely with a moist gauze pad and seek immediate veterinary care.

Proptosis (Eye Out of Socket):
- This is a critical emergency.
- Moisten the exposed eye with sterile saline or water to prevent drying.
- Cover with a moist cloth — do NOT push the eye back.
- Rush to emergency vet immediately.

TOPIC: Ear Injuries in Pets

Common causes: head trauma, foreign bodies (foxtails, grass seeds), hematoma, infections.

Signs: Head shaking, pawing at ear, tilting head, discharge, odour, crying when ear is touched.

What to Do:
1. Do NOT insert objects into the ear canal.
2. If there is visible debris at the outer ear opening, gently wipe away with a damp cloth.
3. For bleeding from the ear flap, apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth.
4. Head shaking with ear hematoma (soft, fluid-filled ear flap): keep the pet calm; the
   hematoma requires veterinary drainage to prevent permanent deformity.
5. Schedule a vet visit for all ear concerns promptly.

SECTION 8: SEIZURES IN PETS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Seizures / Convulsions in Dogs and Cats

Seizures are abnormal, uncontrolled bursts of electrical activity in the brain.
Causes include epilepsy, toxins, low blood sugar, head trauma, and fever.

Phases of a Seizure:
- Pre-ictal (aura): Restlessness, anxiety, hiding — may last seconds to hours
- Ictal (seizure): Muscle spasms, paddling legs, loss of consciousness, salivation,
  urination, defecation — typically lasts 30-90 seconds
- Post-ictal: Confusion, temporary blindness, disorientation, hunger — may last minutes to hours

First Aid During a Seizure:
1. Stay calm. Do NOT put your hand near the mouth — pets CANNOT swallow their tongues
   but CAN bite severely during a seizure.
2. Gently guide your pet away from stairs, furniture edges, or water (ponds, pools).
3. Dim the lights and reduce noise in the room to minimise stimulation.
4. Do NOT restrain the pet — let the seizure run its course.
5. Time the seizure. If it lasts more than 5 minutes (status epilepticus), this is an
   emergency — call your vet immediately while monitoring the pet.
6. If multiple seizures occur within 24 hours (cluster seizures), seek emergency vet care.
7. After the seizure, speak softly and reassure your pet. Offer water once alert.
8. All first-time seizures must be evaluated by a veterinarian.

IMPORTANT: Seizures lasting over 5 minutes cause brain damage and are life-threatening.
Do not wait — go to an emergency vet immediately.

SECTION 9: DROWNING & NEAR-DROWNING IN PETS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Drowning in Dogs and Cats

Even strong swimmers can drown due to exhaustion, currents, or falling into pools.

Signs after near-drowning:
- Coughing, choking, or gasping for air
- Lethargy, pale or blue gums
- Fluid-filled sounds when breathing (pulmonary edema)
- Vomiting water

First Aid for Near-Drowning:
1. Remove the pet from water safely. Do not risk your own drowning.
2. Hold the pet with head lower than body to allow water to drain from lungs.
3. For small pets, gently swing in a downward arc to assist drainage.
4. Check for breathing and heartbeat. Begin rescue breathing or CPR if necessary.
5. Wrap in towels or a blanket to prevent hypothermia.
6. Even if the pet appears to recover, secondary drowning can occur hours later.
7. Transport immediately to emergency vet — all near-drowning cases require medical evaluation.

SECTION 10: BIRTHING EMERGENCIES (DYSTOCIA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Difficult Labour in Pets

Signs that labour requires emergency vet attention:
- Active straining for more than 20-30 minutes without delivery
- Green/black discharge before first puppy/kitten is born
- More than 4 hours between deliveries when more offspring remain
- A puppy/kitten is visibly stuck in the birth canal for more than 10 minutes
- The mother is exhausted, collapsed, or unresponsive

What to Do:
1. Keep the area quiet and warm.
2. Do NOT attempt to pull a stuck offspring unless you are trained to do so.
3. If a kitten or puppy is partially out and stuck for more than 10 minutes, gently
   support (do not pull) and call your vet for instructions.
4. Contact your vet or emergency clinic immediately if any warning signs are present.

SECTION 11: HYPOTHERMIA AND FROSTBITE IN PETS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Hypothermia (Dangerously Low Body Temperature)

Normal body temperature: 38-39.2°C (100.4-102.5°F)
Hypothermia occurs below 36°C (96.8°F)

Signs:
- Shivering (early stage) → stops shivering at very low temps (severe)
- Cold to the touch
- Lethargy, weakness, or stupor
- Fixed, dilated pupils (very severe)

First Aid for Hypothermia:
1. Bring the pet indoors immediately to a warm room.
2. Dry the pet if wet using towels (wet fur dramatically increases heat loss).
3. Wrap in warm (not hot) towels or blankets. A hot water bottle wrapped in cloth
   can be placed next to (not against) the body.
4. Do NOT use a hairdryer on high heat — this can burn the skin.
5. Offer warm (not hot) water to drink if conscious.
6. Monitor temperature and seek veterinary care — underlying causes must be treated.

TOPIC: Frostbite in Pets

Frostbite most commonly affects ears, tail, paws, and scrotum.

Signs: White, grey, or blue-tinged skin. Painful or numb to touch. Blistering or peeling.

What to Do:
1. Move pet to a warm environment.
2. Warm affected areas with warm (not hot) water (approximately 40°C/104°F).
3. Do NOT rub frostbitten skin — rubbing causes tissue damage.
4. Do NOT apply direct heat sources.
5. After warming, cover loosely with clean bandage and seek veterinary care promptly.

SECTION 12: INSECT STINGS AND ANAPHYLAXIS IN PETS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TOPIC: Bee Stings, Wasp Stings, and Allergic Reactions

Pets are often stung on the face, mouth, or paws.

Signs of Local Reaction:
- Swelling, redness, or hives at sting site
- Licking or scratching

Signs of Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis):
- Rapid swelling of face, throat, or muzzle
- Difficulty breathing or wheezing
- Pale or white gums
- Vomiting, diarrhoea
- Sudden collapse or loss of consciousness

First Aid for Bee Stings:
1. Remove the stinger by scraping sideways with a credit card — do NOT squeeze with
   tweezers, this injects more venom.
2. Apply a cold compress to reduce swelling.
3. Monitor your pet closely for 30-60 minutes.
4. For mild swelling: contact your vet — they may advise an antihistamine dose.
5. For ANY signs of anaphylaxis: this is a life-threatening emergency — go to an
   emergency vet immediately. Do not wait to see if symptoms improve.

EMERGENCY CONTACTS AND CLOSING NOTES

Emergency Contacts:
- ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center: +1-888-426-4435 (Available 24/7, US)
- Pet Poison Helpline: +1-855-764-7661 (US)
- Always have your local veterinarian's emergency number saved in your phone.

General First-Aid Principles for Pets:
1. Your safety comes first. An injured animal in pain may bite or scratch.
2. Keep calm — panic worsens the situation for you and your pet.
3. Minimise movement of an injured animal during transport.
4. Keep injured pets warm and quiet.
5. NEVER give human medications to pets unless explicitly directed by a vet.
6. When in doubt — call a vet. Do not rely solely on this guide for serious emergencies.
7. This guide is a reference tool only and does NOT replace professional veterinary care.
